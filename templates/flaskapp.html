Traffic Accidents

Fostering tomorrow’s safe roads by sharing insights about traffic accidents and their patterns

About

Project Intro & Goals

This project analyzes data about vehicle accidents in the US, UK and France with the goal of informing users about patterns in these accidents and potentially how to prevent them. Users of this tool might include governmental authorities and organizations, such as the Department of Transportation in the US, the Secretary of State for Transport in the UK, and the Minister of Transportation in France. These government bodies might use these visualizations to understand the circumstances around traffic accidents, which might help them develop new policies or regulations to improve road safety. This tool is also available to the general public so that they can make informed decisions about where or when they drive and under what conditions, if they have flexibility to choose. While this project is not a study of causation, we hope that by understanding correlations in traffic accidents, our users can understand the circumstances around more frequent and more severe accidents.

Datasets

Our datasets combined vary over time, with the US spanning from 2016 to 2021, the UK from 2005 to 2014 (except 2008), and France from 2005 to 2016. We obtained these datasets through Kaggle, and they can be found here: US dataset, UK dataset, France dataset.

Data Cleaning

We had to clean and process these datasets in order to get them in a usable format to draw conclusions from intranational accidents and to make international comparisons. For example, in the UK dataset, we had to drop duplicate accidents and merge separate datasets across years to create one continuous dataset. For France, we merged separate datasets with different accident characteristics, including holidays, places, users, and vehicles. Finally, to combine the separate datasets for each country together, we identified common features across all countries and standardized the values to make comparisons. There were three themes of features that were found in each country: time-based features, weather data, and road type information. These themes inspired three of our Tableau visualization dashboards.

Accident Severity

Additionally, each dataset had a measurement of accident severity, which had varying scales for each country. We standardized this feature across the countries to allow users to understand correlations between accident conditions and severity. The US’s severity metric actually measured the accident’s impact on traffic, which we assumed could be generalized to the severity of the accident itself. The US and France measured severity on a scale from 1 to 4, while the UK measured severity on a scale from 1 to 3. For consistency, we converted all countries to a 1 to 3 scale with the following interpretations:

  • 1: minor accident
  • 2: severe accident
  • 3: extreme or fatal accident

Variable Types

  • Weather: What was the weather when the accident occurred?
  • Road Type: What type of road did the accident take place on?
  • Road Condition: What was the condition of the road that the accident took place on?
  • Light: What time of day did the accident occur?

Visualizations

Time

These graphs below display the number of accidents across different time frames. The first graph shows the number of accidents by month, color-coded by year. The bottom left graph shows the percent of accidents in a country based on the day of week. The bottom right graph shows the percent of accidents in a country based on the hour of day. Click on the Weather, Light, Road Type, and Road Condition filters to view the correlation of such variables on accident frequency.



Weather

In the Tableau dashboard below, we analyze correlations between accident severity and different weather conditions, including the weather itself (ex. clear, foggy, rainy, snowy, etc.), lighting conditions (ex. day versus night), and road conditions (ex. dry, flooded, icy, snowy, or wet). There is some correlation between weather and road conditions–for example, when it is rainy, the road is most likely wet–but we included both conditions since the US was entirely missing road conditions but the road conditions still provided a unique perspective about what drivers are facing on the road. These histograms compare accident severity across different conditions. Each histogram chart has a “baseline” category: clear for the weather histogram, daylight for the light histogram, and dry for the road conditions histogram. The bars represent the percent change in average severity compared to this baseline condition. For example, in France, foggy weather conditions are on average 17% more severe than clear conditions. Government officials in France might see this observation and conclude that it could be helpful for vehicles in France to have better headlights that allow drivers to see better through fog.
